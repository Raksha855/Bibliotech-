How a Bill Becomes a Law in India

“A bill is just an idea until Parliament gives it the breath of law.” — Bibliotech

Types of Bills

Type Description
Ordinary Bill Any matter except money/finance and Constitutional amendments
Money Bill (Art. 110) Deals with taxes, borrowing, Consolidated Fund — introduced only in Lok Sabha
Financial Bill Includes both financial and non-financial provisions
Constitution Amendment Bill Amends Constitution under Article 368
Private Member Bill Introduced by any MP other than a minister

Procedure for Ordinary Bill

  1. First Reading: Introduction of the bill (no debate)
  2. Second Reading:
    • Detailed discussion
    • Referred to committee (optional)
  3. Third Reading: Final voting and passage
  4. Other House: Same 3 readings occur
  5. President's Assent:
    • Can give assent
    • Withhold assent
    • Return (only once, not for Money Bill)

Procedure for Money Bill (Article 110)

  • Introduced only in Lok Sabha (by minister)
  • Rajya Sabha can only make recommendations (within 14 days)
  • Lok Sabha may accept or reject recommendations
  • President cannot return Money Bill for reconsideration

Procedure for Constitution Amendment (Article 368)

  • Introduced in either House
  • Requires special majority in both Houses
  • In some cases, ratification by half the states is needed
  • No need for President’s approval (but traditionally given)

Flow Summary

  1. Idea → Drafted → Introduced
  2. Discussed → Voted → Passed in one House
  3. Sent to other House → Same process
  4. President → Becomes Law

UPSC Notes

  • Joint Sitting: Article 108 — Only for Ordinary Bills (not Money or Constitutional Amendment)
  • Deadlock resolution: President can call joint session
  • Money Bill dispute: Speaker's decision is final
