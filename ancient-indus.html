Indus Valley Civilization (Harappan Culture)

"A civilization ahead of its time — with drainage smarter than many modern towns." — Bibliotech

What was the Indus Valley Civilization?

Also known as the **Harappan Civilization**, it was one of the **world’s earliest urban civilizations**, contemporary with Mesopotamia and Egypt. It flourished from **2600 BCE to 1900 BCE** in the northwestern regions of South Asia.

Major Sites of IVC

  • Harappa: First discovered site (Punjab, Pakistan) — granaries, cemetery R-37
  • Mohenjo-Daro: Planned city (Sindh, Pakistan) — Great Bath, drainage system
  • Dholavira: (Gujarat) — Water reservoirs, signboard in Harappan script
  • Lothal: (Gujarat) — Dockyard, trade center
  • Kalibangan: (Rajasthan) — Fire altars, ploughed field
  • Rakhigarhi: (Haryana) — Largest site in India

Town Planning & Architecture

  • Well-planned cities with grid patterns
  • Advanced drainage system
  • Baked brick houses, public baths, granaries
  • Division of city into **Citadel (Upper town)** and **Lower town**

Economy

  • Agriculture: wheat, barley, cotton (first in the world!)
  • Domestication: cattle, sheep, goats
  • Trade: internal & external (Mesopotamia - via seals)
  • Use of standardized weights & measures

Art & Culture

  • Terracotta figurines (Mother Goddess, bull)
  • Seals (Pashupati seal, unicorn motif)
  • Beads, bangles, pottery, bronze dancing girl
  • No evidence of temples or kings

Script

  • Pictographic and undeciphered
  • Written from right to left (mostly)

Decline Theories

  • Ecological changes: drying of rivers (Saraswati), floods
  • Aryan invasion theory (controversial & outdated)
  • Decline in trade and agriculture

Important UPSC Notes

  • First city excavated: Harappa (1921 by Daya Ram Sahni)
  • City with Great Bath: Mohenjo-Daro
  • City with Dockyard: Lothal
  • City with Water Reservoir: Dholavira
  • City with Fire Altars: Kalibangan

UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Sites, features, excavators, maps
  • Mains: GS1 – Urban planning, IVC vs Mesopotamia
  • Essay: “The Genius of the Indus: A Lost Urban Legacy”
