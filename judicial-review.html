Judicial Review in India

“Judicial review is the backbone of our constitutional democracy, ensuring that legislation remains within the bounds of the Constitution.” — Bibliotech

Overview

Judicial review is the power vested in the judiciary, especially the Supreme Court, to review and invalidate legislative and executive actions that are inconsistent with the Constitution. It serves as a key mechanism to safeguard the fundamental rights of citizens and maintain the rule of law.

Key Features

  • Ensures that laws and governmental actions conform to constitutional provisions.
  • Acts as a check on the arbitrary exercise of power by the legislature and the executive.
  • Empowers citizens to seek redress when their fundamental rights are breached.

Important Constitutional Provisions

  • Article 32: Grants the right to constitutional remedies and enables citizens to approach the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.
  • Article 136: Provides the Supreme Court with the discretion to grant special leave to appeal from any judgment or order in any matter.

Landmark Cases

  • Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973): Established the Basic Structure doctrine, limiting Parliament’s power to amend the Constitution.
  • Minerva Mills Ltd. vs. Union of India (1980): Emphasized the need for balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles.
  • Shayara Bano vs. Union of India (2017): Demonstrated the power of judicial review by striking down unconstitutional provisions.

Judicial Impact

Judicial review protects the constitutional framework by ensuring that even emergency measures and legislative actions are subject to the supremacy of the Constitution. It remains a cornerstone in upholding the democratic values and rights of the citizens.
