British Expansion in India

"The British didn’t conquer India in a day — they did it one treaty, one battle, and one betrayal at a time." — Bibliotech

Introduction

After gaining economic and political control over Bengal (Plassey & Buxar), the British East India Company began expanding across the Indian subcontinent using military power, diplomacy, and clever policies.

Methods of Expansion

  • Subsidiary Alliance – introduced by Lord Wellesley
  • Doctrine of Lapse – introduced by Lord Dalhousie
  • Direct conquest through wars
  • Treaties, alliances & annexations

Major Conquests & Wars

Region/Kingdom Conflict/War Result
Mysore Anglo-Mysore Wars (1767–1799) Tipu Sultan defeated & killed in 4th War (1799)
Marathas Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818) Maratha Confederacy broken; British supremacy in Deccan
Sikhs Anglo-Sikh Wars (1845–1849) Punjab annexed after Second Anglo-Sikh War
Awadh Annexed in 1856 On pretext of misgovernance by Nawab Wajid Ali Shah

Subsidiary Alliance (1798)

  • Introduced by Lord Wellesley.
  • Indian ruler had to maintain British troops at his cost.
  • Could not make alliances or declare war without British permission.
  • First signed by Nizam of Hyderabad (1798).

Doctrine of Lapse (1848)

  • Introduced by Lord Dalhousie.
  • If an Indian ruler died without a natural heir, his kingdom would be annexed.
  • Adopted sons were not recognized as legal heirs.
  • Annexed states: Satara, Jhansi, Nagpur, Udaipur.

Impact of British Expansion

  • Political unification of India under one power for first time.
  • Rise of discontent among Indian rulers & people.
  • Increased economic exploitation & drain of resources.
  • Laid groundwork for 1857 Revolt.

UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Policies (Doctrine of Lapse, Subsidiary Alliance), important wars.
  • Mains GS1: How the Company transformed into a ruling power.
  • Essay: “Empire by trick and treaty: The story of British conquest.”

British Administration in India

“From a trading company to a sovereign power—British India was governed by Acts, councils, and collectors.” — Bibliotech

1. Evolution of Authority

  • Company Rule (1600–1858):
    • East India Company governed vast territories under successive Charter Acts.
    • Regulating Act (1773): Governor of Bengal & Council; Supreme Court at Calcutta.
    • Pitt’s India Act (1784): Board of Control established to oversee Company.
    • Charter Acts (1813, 1833, 1853): Gradual centralization; elimination of Company’s commercial rights; Governor-General of India (1833).
  • Crown Rule (1858–1947):
    • Government of India Act (1858): Secretary of State for India in London; India Council.
    • Viceroy & Governor-General of India appointed by Crown.
    • Indian Councils Acts & Government of India Acts (1861, 1892, 1909, 1919, 1935) expanded legislative bodies.

2. Central Administration

AuthorityRole
Secretary of State for India Oversaw Indian affairs in London; advised by India Council
Viceroy & Governor‑General Chief executive in India; commanded army; presided over Governor‑General’s Council
Executive Council Advised Viceroy; oversaw departments (Revenue, Military, Law, Home, Finance)
Legislative Council, India Made laws for British India; membership expanded over time

3. Provincial Administration

  • Governors & Lieutenant‑Governors: Headed Presidencies (Bombay, Madras, Bengal) & Provinces.
  • Executive Councils: Assisted Governors; controlled finances & policing.
  • Provincial Legislative Councils: Introduced by Acts of 1861, 1892; more elected Indian members under Morley‑Minto (1909) & Montagu‑Chelmsford (1919).

4. District Administration

  • District Collector/Magistrate:
    • Revenue collection, law and order, public works.
    • Combined roles split post‑1935 between District Magistrate (law & order) and District Collector (revenue).
  • Sub‑Divisional Officers: Assisted Collector; handled magisterial duties in subdivisions.
  • Tehsildars & Kanungos: Broken down revenue work at taluk/tehsil level.

5. Judicial System

  • Supreme Court (1774): At Calcutta; jurisdiction over Company servants and Europeans.
  • Sadar Diwani & Nizamat Adalats (1780): Civil and criminal appellate courts for Indians.
  • High Courts (1861): Established at Calcutta, Bombay, Madras; replaced Supreme & Sadar Adalats.
  • Subordinate Courts: District & Sessions Courts; subordinate to High Courts; regulated by Indian High Courts Act (1861).

6. Police & Revenue Departments

  • Police Act (Police Act 1861): Created Inspector General, District Superintendents; structured police forces.
  • Revenue Department: Overseen by Board of Revenue at Presidency level; District Collector as chief revenue officer.

7. Local Self‑Government

  • Municipalities (1850s): First in Madras (1864), Bombay & Calcutta soon after; elected Indian members gradually added.
  • District Boards (1882): Established under Lord Ripon; rural local bodies for public works, education.
  • Expansion (1919, 1935): Greater autonomy and financial powers to local bodies.

8. Legislative Reforms

  • Indian Councils Act 1861: Introduced non‑official members in Central & Provincial councils.
  • Acts of 1892 & 1909: Expanded councils; introduced separate electorates for Muslims.
  • Government of India Act 1919: Dyarchy in provinces; dual control of subjects.
  • Government of India Act 1935: Provincial autonomy; bicameral legislatures in some provinces; federal scheme (never implemented fully).

9. UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Key acts, titles (Viceroy, Secretary of State), structures.
  • Mains GS2: Evolution of governance, colonial institutions, administrative legacy.
  • Essay: “Colonial Administration: Foundation for Modern India?”
