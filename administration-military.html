Administration & Military System in Medieval India

"Medieval India's strength lay not only in its sword, but in the art of ruling diverse peoples." — Bibliotech

Introduction

From the Delhi Sultanate to the Mughal Empire, the administration and military systems of medieval India saw remarkable evolution. These systems laid the foundation for governance, taxation, revenue collection, law enforcement, and defense mechanisms that influenced later Indian polity.

Administrative Features of the Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526)

Central Administration

  • Sultan: The supreme head of the empire, both religiously and politically.
  • Naib (Deputy Sultan): Assisted in central authority in Sultan’s absence.
  • Wazir: Head of finance department — responsible for revenue, treasury.
  • Ariz-i-Mumalik: Head of military affairs, recruitment, salaries, equipment.
  • Qazi: Judicial head, overseer of Islamic laws (Sharia).
  • Barid-i-Mumalik: Head of intelligence and espionage system.

Provincial Administration

  • Empire divided into Iqtas; governed by Muqtis or Walis.
  • Muqtis collected land revenue and maintained law and order.
  • Diwan-i-Wizarat: Maintained revenue records from provinces.

Judicial System

  • Based on Islamic law (Shariat), with the Qazi acting as chief judge.
  • Criminal justice was harsh — corporal punishment, public execution.
  • Secular matters judged through local customs in villages.

Revenue System

  • Land tax was the primary source of income.
  • Kharaj: Land tax (around 1/5 of produce).
  • Revenue often collected in cash or kind.

Mughal Administration (1526–1857)

Central Government

  • Padshah (Emperor): Head of executive, legislative, judiciary, and military powers.
  • Diwan-i-Ala: Revenue minister (like Wazir).
  • Mir Bakshi: Head of military recruitment and mansabdari system.
  • Sadr-us-Sudur: Religious and charitable affairs.
  • Mir Saman: Head of imperial household and royal stores.

Provincial Administration

  • Empire divided into Subas (provinces).
  • Each Suba had: Subedar (governor), Diwan (finance), Bakshi (military), Qazi (law).
  • Faujdars: Maintained law & order and supervised jagirdars.

Mansabdari System (by Akbar)

  • Mansab: Rank granted to civil/military officials.
  • Zat: Personal rank/salary; Sawar: Cavalry obligation.
  • Officers given Jagirs (land assignments) in return for service — **not hereditary**.

Revenue System

  • Raja Todar Mal’s Zabt System: Standardized measurement & assessment.
  • Classification of land into Polaj, Parauti, Chachar, Banjar.
  • Cash payments encouraged for accountability.

Justice System

  • Qazis handled Islamic cases, while Hindus followed their traditional laws.
  • Appeals went directly to the Emperor’s court.

Military System of Medieval India

Delhi Sultanate

  • Ariz-i-Mumalik: Responsible for training, recruiting, and supplying arms.
  • Troops recruited directly or supplied by nobles/muqtis.
  • Use of Turkish horsemen, archers, cavalry units.
  • Introduction of metal armor, catapults, siege tactics.

Mughal Military

  • Mansabdars maintained cavalry: Horses branded (dagh system) for authenticity.
  • Artillery: Use of cannons (Babur at Panipat), matchlocks, rockets later.
  • Elephants: Used as royal parade and in battles.
  • Navy: Weak compared to European powers (British, Portuguese).

Military Technologies

  • Gunpowder revolutionized battles — first used by Babur (1526).
  • Fort architecture: Red Fort, Gwalior Fort, Tughlaqabad, Agra Fort.

Comparative Analysis: Delhi Sultanate vs Mughal Administration

Feature Delhi Sultanate Mughal Empire
Land Revenue Kharaj (fixed portion) Zabt system, crop survey
Military System Muqti-based; no permanent army Mansabdari with cavalry requirements
Judiciary Islamic law dominance More tolerance for local laws
Religious Policy Islamic supremacy Sulh-i-Kul under Akbar
