Amendment of the Constitution (Article 368)

“A Constitution must be flexible to grow, and firm enough to hold the nation together.” — Bibliotech

What is a Constitutional Amendment?

An amendment is a formal change made to the Constitution to adapt it to changing times and needs. In India, the procedure is detailed under Article 368 of the Constitution.

Types of Amendments (Article 368)

Type Procedure Examples
1. By Simple Majority Outside Article 368 (passed like ordinary bill) Creation of new states, salaries of MPs
2. By Special Majority 2/3rd of members present + voting & majority of total members of each House Abolition of Right to Property, GST Amendment
3. Special Majority + State Ratification Special majority + approval by half of the states Election of President, distribution of powers

Procedure for Amendment (Under Article 368)

  1. Bill introduced in either House of Parliament
  2. Passed in each House by Special Majority
  3. For some provisions, approval of at least half of the state legislatures needed
  4. Sent to President for assent
  5. No provision for referendum

Important Constitutional Amendments

  • 1st Amendment (1951): Reasonable restrictions on rights
  • 42nd Amendment (1976): Mini-Constitution; added Socialist, Secular, Integrity
  • 44th Amendment (1978): Restored FRs curtailed in Emergency
  • 73rd & 74th Amendments: Panchayati Raj and Municipalities
  • 86th Amendment: Made education a Fundamental Right (Art. 21A)
  • 101st Amendment: GST introduced

Judicial Interpretation

  • Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973): Parliament can amend any part but cannot alter Basic Structure
  • Minerva Mills Case (1980): Balance between FRs and DPSPs is part of Basic Structure

Basic Structure Doctrine

This doctrine ensures that Parliament's power to amend the Constitution is limited by certain fundamental features like:

  • Supremacy of the Constitution
  • Sovereign, Democratic & Republic nature
  • Secularism, Separation of powers, Federalism
  • Judicial review, Fundamental Rights
