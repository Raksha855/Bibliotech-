Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP) – Introduction

Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV, Articles 36–51) are the ideals that the State should strive to achieve to establish a just society. They are non-justiciable but fundamental in the governance of the country.

Origin & Background

  • Inspired by the Irish Constitution (1937)
  • Also influenced by Gandhian principles & Socialist ideals
  • Inserted by the Drafting Committee under Dr. Ambedkar

Features of DPSP

  • Not enforceable by any court (non-justiciable)
  • State must keep them in mind while making laws
  • Aim: Social and Economic democracy
  • Supplement Fundamental Rights (Political democracy)

Classification of DPSPs (as per content)

  • 1. Socialist Principles – Aim at social & economic equality
  • 2. Gandhian Principles – Reflect Gandhi's philosophy
  • 3. Liberal-Intellectual Principles – Western ideals like international peace

Examples:

  • Equal pay for equal work – Article 39(d)
  • Free legal aid – Article 39A
  • Organization of village panchayats – Article 40
  • Uniform Civil Code – Article 44
