Constitutional Development in India (1773–1947)

“A constitution is the backbone of a nation — and India's evolved through a long journey of trials, acts, and awakening.” — Bibliotech

1. Regulating Act of 1773

  • First step by British Parliament to regulate East India Company’s affairs.
  • Governor of Bengal became Governor-General of Bengal (Warren Hastings).
  • Established Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774).

2. Pitt’s India Act of 1784

  • Established dual control — political affairs under Board of Control, commercial affairs under Court of Directors.
  • Increased Crown's control over Company.

3. Charter Acts (1793–1853)

  • 1813: Ended East India Company’s trade monopoly.
  • 1833: Governor-General of India (William Bentinck); Company became purely administrative body.
  • 1853: Introduced open competition for civil services; legislative powers separated from executive.

4. Government of India Act, 1858

  • After Revolt of 1857, power transferred to the British Crown.
  • Secretary of State for India created; Governor-General became Viceroy (Lord Canning first).

5. Indian Councils Acts

  • 1861: Introduced legislative councils for provinces.
  • 1892: First introduction of indirect elections in legislative councils.
  • 1909 (Morley-Minto Reforms): Introduced separate electorates for Muslims, expanded councils.

6. Government of India Act, 1919 (Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms)

  • Introduced Dyarchy in Provinces (Transferred & Reserved subjects).
  • Bicameral legislature at center — Central Legislative Assembly & Council of States.
  • Franchise expanded but still limited.

7. Government of India Act, 1935

  • Most comprehensive act before Independence.
  • Proposed All-India Federation (never implemented).
  • Provincial Autonomy — provinces got elected governments.
  • Separate electorates continued; federal court established.

8. Indian Independence Act, 1947

  • Passed by British Parliament — ended British rule in India.
  • Two dominions created: India and Pakistan.
  • Governor-General continued till Constitution was adopted.

9. UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Key acts and years, first Governor-General, dyarchy, federal court, separate electorates.
  • Mains GS2: Evolution of Indian polity, colonial legacy in constitutional framework.
  • Essay: “India’s constitutional evolution: From Regulating Act to Republic.”
Important Note: Most provisions of the 1935 Act were later adopted in our Constitution (1950), especially federal structure, emergency powers, and administrative set-up.

Further Insights into Constitutional Development in India

“The evolution of India’s Constitution is not a chapter — it is an epic.” — Bibliotech

1. Evolution from Company Rule to Crown Rule

  • Regulating Act (1773) was a response to the financial crisis & corruption in East India Company.
  • Pitt’s India Act (1784) clarified the duality of power — Company was administrator but Parliament had control.
  • 1858 marked the direct intervention of the Crown — beginning of Viceroy-led administration.

2. Rise of Legislative Consciousness

  • The Indian Councils Act of 1861 marked the start of Indian representation in legislation.
  • By 1892 and 1909, nationalist pressure led to limited electoral provisions and expanded Indian membership.
  • Though superficial, these reforms sowed seeds of political awakening.

3. British Strategy Behind Reforms

The colonial strategy was clear — “Divide and Rule.”
  • Separate electorates (1909) institutionalized communal divisions.
  • Dyarchy (1919) was a confusing system — power without real authority.
  • The 1935 Act gave autonomy but retained ultimate control with the Governor-General.

4. Role of Indian Leaders in Shaping Constitutional Demands

  • Dadabhai Naoroji: Demanded self-governance, exposed economic drain.
  • Bal Gangadhar Tilak: “Swaraj is my birthright” became a constitutional demand.
  • Nehru Report (1928): First Indian effort to draft a constitution.
  • Gandhi: Mass movements pushed British into negotiating terms of constitutional reform (Round Table Conferences).

5. Cripps Mission & Cabinet Mission

  • Cripps Mission (1942): Offered Dominion Status after war — rejected due to weak terms.
  • Cabinet Mission (1946): Proposed united India with groupings of provinces — accepted initially but failed later.

6. Legacy of Colonial Constitution-Making

  • Federal structure, civil services, rule of law — inherited from British system.
  • Communal electorates led to partition, but also formed the basis of debate on secularism in post-Independence India.
  • 1935 Act served as backbone of the Indian Constitution (over 250 provisions borrowed).
Did You Know?
Indian Constituent Assembly was not fully elected through adult suffrage. It was partly nominated and partly indirectly elected by provincial assemblies.

  • Essay: “From subjects to citizens: Constitutional journey of India.”
  • Interview: Questions on dyarchy, 1935 Act’s relevance today, impact of separate electorates.
