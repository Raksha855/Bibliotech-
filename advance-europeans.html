Advent of Europeans in India

“When spices were more valuable than gold, the seas became battlefields of empires.” — Bibliotech

Introduction

The arrival of Europeans in India marked the beginning of a new era. Starting in the 15th century, several European powers reached Indian shores primarily for trade, but eventually laid the foundation for colonization.

Why Did Europeans Come to India?

  • To break the monopoly of Arab traders over the spice trade.
  • To find a direct sea route to India after the fall of Constantinople (1453).
  • Economic motives: India was known for its spices, textiles, gems, and handicrafts.
  • Religious motives: Spread of Christianity (especially for Portuguese).
  • Political expansion of empires like Portugal, Spain, Britain, and France.

1. Portuguese in India

  • First to arrive, last to leave (1498–1961).
  • Vasco da Gama landed at Calicut (1498), welcomed by Zamorin.
  • Established strongholds: Goa (1510), Diu, Daman, Bassein, and Cochin.
  • Francisco de Almeida: Policy of Blue Water (naval dominance).
  • Alfonso de Albuquerque: Captured Goa, encouraged intermarriage, allowed conversion.
  • Introduced: Tobacco, Cashew, and Printing Press in India (Goa).

2. Dutch in India

  • Established by the Dutch East India Company (1602).
  • Focused on spice trade in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Bengal.
  • Lost influence after defeat in the Battle of Colachel (1741) by Travancore King Marthanda Varma.
  • Eventually ousted by the British and withdrew to Indonesia.

3. English in India

  • East India Company received charter from Queen Elizabeth I in 1600.
  • First factory: Surat (1613), allowed by Mughal Emperor Jahangir.
  • Important settlements: Bombay (1668), Madras (1639), Calcutta (1690).
  • Fortified trading posts: Fort St. George (Madras), Fort William (Calcutta), Bombay Castle.

4. French in India

  • French East India Company founded in 1664.
  • Established Pondicherry, Chandernagore, Mahe, and Karaikal.
  • Frequent conflicts with British → **Carnatic Wars (1746–1763)**.
  • French permanently lost political ambitions in India after defeat in the Battle of Wandiwash (1760).

5. Danish in India

  • Established at Tranquebar (Tamil Nadu) in 1620.
  • Trade focus on pepper and textiles.
  • Sold their possessions to the British in 1845 and withdrew.

Conclusion

While many European powers entered India for trade, it was the British who ultimately gained political dominance due to superior military tactics, naval strength, and diplomatic strategies.

UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Years of arrival, battles (Wandiwash, Colachel), key ports.
  • Mains GS1: Impact of European advent, beginning of colonization.
  • Essay: “When trade became conquest: Europe in India.”

The Carnatic Wars (1746–1763)

“The Carnatic Wars were not just about trade — they were the rehearsal of colonial conquest.” — Bibliotech

What were the Carnatic Wars?

A series of military conflicts in the Carnatic region of South India, fought mainly between the **British East India Company** and the **French East India Company**, with Indian rulers as allies. These wars decided the fate of European colonialism in India.

Background

  • Both British and French companies had trading posts in South India.
  • They allied with local Nawabs to expand influence.
  • The wars were connected to larger global conflicts between Britain and France.

1️⃣ First Carnatic War (1746–1748)

  • Part of the War of Austrian Succession in Europe.
  • French captured **Madras** from British (1746).
  • **Battle of St. Thomé:** Nawab of Carnatic attacked French, but defeated by Dupleix.
  • Ended by the **Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)** → Madras returned to British.

2️⃣ Second Carnatic War (1749–1754)

  • Political conflict over succession in Hyderabad and Carnatic.
  • French supported Chanda Sahib & Muzaffar Jung.
  • British supported Muhammad Ali and Nasir Jang.
  • **Robert Clive** emerged as a military genius (Battle of Arcot – 1751).
  • Result: British-backed Muhammad Ali became Nawab of Carnatic.
  • French influence declined, though still present.

3️⃣ Third Carnatic War (1757–1763)

  • Coincided with **Seven Years’ War** in Europe.
  • Decisive war for supremacy in India.
  • **Battle of Wandiwash (1760):** British (Sir Eyre Coote) defeated French (Count Lally).
  • French lost Pondicherry (captured by British), and most trading posts.
  • Ended with **Treaty of Paris (1763)** → French lost political influence, retained trading rights only.

Major Battles Summary

Battle Year Result
Battle of St. Thomé 1746 French victory over Nawab of Carnatic
Battle of Arcot 1751 British victory, rise of Clive
Battle of Wandiwash 1760 Decisive British victory

Why Did British Win?

  • Stronger naval power and reinforcements.
  • Efficient leadership (Robert Clive, Eyre Coote).
  • Control over local revenue resources.
  • Better diplomacy with Indian rulers.

Aftermath & Significance

  • French influence limited to trading posts — no political ambitions allowed.
  • British became dominant European power in India.
  • Paved the way for British territorial conquest — starting with Bengal (Battle of Plassey 1757).

UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Dates, battles, leaders, treaties.
  • Mains GS1: Role of European powers, strategic colonization.
  • Essay: “When commerce clashed with cannons: Carnatic Wars and colonial destiny.”

Battle of Plassey (1757) & Battle of Buxar (1764)

"A handful of merchants defeated mighty Nawabs — and wrote the first chapter of British Empire in India." — Bibliotech

Battle of Plassey – 1757

Background

  • British East India Company wanted trade concessions & security in Bengal.
  • Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah opposed British fortifications in Calcutta.
  • British captured Calcutta after Siraj’s attack (Black Hole Tragedy).
  • Company allied with Mir Jafar, bankers (Jagat Seth), and others to remove Siraj.

Battle Details

  • Date: 23 June 1757
  • Place: Plassey (Nadia district, West Bengal)
  • British led by: Robert Clive
  • Nawab: Siraj-ud-Daulah
  • Key Betrayal: Mir Jafar withheld his army during battle.
  • Result: British victory due to treachery, not military superiority.

Significance of Plassey

  • British became virtual rulers of Bengal.
  • Mir Jafar installed as puppet Nawab.
  • Company gained huge wealth — beginning of Drain of Wealth.
  • Laid foundation for political expansion.

Battle of Buxar – 1764

Background

  • Mir Qasim (successor of Mir Jafar) tried to assert independence.
  • He formed an alliance with Nawab of Awadh (Shuja-ud-Daula) and Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II.
  • Wanted to challenge British dominance.

Battle Details

  • Date: 22 October 1764
  • Place: Buxar (Bihar)
  • British led by: Hector Munro
  • Allied Indian powers defeated.

Significance of Buxar

  • British gained military supremacy over northern India.
  • Established themselves as major power over Mughals and Nawabs.
  • Treaty of Allahabad (1765):
    • Company got Diwani rights (right to collect revenue) of Bengal, Bihar, Orissa.
    • Shah Alam II given Rs. 26 lakh annually.
    • Company now ruled economically & militarily.
