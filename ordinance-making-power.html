Ordinance-Making Power in India

“Ordinances are the temporary laws when the legislature sleeps; they are meant to keep the machinery of governance running in extraordinary times.” – Bibliotech

Overview

The ordinance-making power is a unique executive power granted to the President under Article 123 of the Indian Constitution. It allows the President, on the advice of the Council of Ministers, to promulgate temporary laws when either House of Parliament is not in session.

Procedure & Key Features

  • Initiation: The Council of Ministers recommends the issuance of an ordinance to the President.
  • Proclamation: The President promulgates the ordinance, making it an act of law.
  • Duration: The ordinance remains in force until Parliament reconvenes and passes it as a law, or it expires after 6 weeks of reassembly.
  • Control: It is subject to subsequent approval by both Houses of Parliament.
  • Limitation: Ordinances cannot be used to amend the Constitution, and they must be temporary in nature.

Flowchart of the Process

Step Description
1. Need Arises The government requires immediate action when Parliament is not in session.
2. Ministerial Advice The Council of Ministers advises the President to issue an ordinance.
3. Promulgation The President issues the ordinance, which becomes law immediately.
4. Parliamentary Action Within 6 weeks of Parliament reconvening, the ordinance must be approved or it lapses.

Significance & UPSC Relevance

  • Ensures continuous governance in emergencies or when Parliament is adjourned.
  • Provides a check on the flexibility and power of the executive.
  • Frequently discussed in UPSC GS II and Polity, especially regarding checks and balances in a democracy.
  • Case studies and debates around ordinance usage (e.g., controversies during legislative adjournments) are pertinent for essays and ethics.

Judicial Perspective

The judiciary has often scrutinized the excessive and prolonged use of ordinances to ensure they do not become a substitute for proper legislative procedure. The principle is that ordinances are temporary and must not undermine the Parliament's law-making authority.
