Harshavardhana & Pushyabhuti Dynasty

"He lost battles but won hearts — Harsha, the philosopher king of the north." — Bibliotech

Origin of Pushyabhuti Dynasty

  • Started as a minor feudal dynasty in Thanesar (modern Haryana)
  • Prabhakaravardhana — expanded the kingdom and declared independence from Guptas
  • Sons: Rajyavardhana (elder) and Harshavardhana (younger)

Harshavardhana (606–647 CE)

  • Came to power after the murder of his brother Rajyavardhana
  • Capital: Kanauj
  • Conquered Bengal, Orissa, Assam, and parts of Central India
  • Defeated by Pulakesin II (Chalukya ruler of Deccan) on the Narmada river

Administration

  • Decentralized polity with feudal elements
  • Issued land grants to Brahmins and officials
  • Maintained a large army
  • Held Religious Assemblies and Poet Conventions

Religion & Patronage

  • Practiced Shaivism but showed religious tolerance
  • Supported Buddhism as well — built monasteries and rest houses
  • Organized Kannauj Assembly (643 CE) attended by Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang)

Xuanzang’s Account

  • Describes Harsha as a just and noble ruler
  • Mentioned Nalanda University and India's prosperity

Literature & Culture

  • Harsha himself was a great scholar and playwright
  • Wrote: Ratnavali, Nagananda, Priyadarshika (Sanskrit plays)
  • Banabhatta — his court poet, author of Harshacharita and Kadambari

Decline

  • Died without an heir — kingdom fragmented
  • Marked the beginning of Early Medieval India (regional kingdoms rise)

UPSC Relevance

  • Prelims: Xuanzang visit, plays, battles, capital
  • Mains GS1: Transition from ancient to medieval India
  • Essay: “Harsha: The king who balanced sword and scripture”
