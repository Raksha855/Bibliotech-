Judicial Interpretation of the Preamble

The Preamble has been extensively interpreted by the Supreme Court in several landmark cases. Let’s explore how the judiciary has defined its role and importance.

1. Berubari Union Case (1960)

- Supreme Court held that the Preamble is not a part of the Constitution. - It cannot be enforced in a court of law. - It is useful for interpreting ambiguous provisions of the Constitution.

2. Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)

- A landmark judgment that overturned the Berubari view. - Supreme Court ruled: Preamble is a part of the Constitution. - It reflects the basic structure and cannot be amended in violation of the basic features.

3. LIC of India Case (1995)

- Reaffirmed that Preamble is part of the Constitution. - It expresses the philosophy and aims of the Constitution. - Not directly enforceable, but still important in constitutional interpretation.

4. Union of India vs. Madangopal (1954)

- Court held that the Preamble is key in understanding the spirit of the Constitution. - It helps in resolving doubts in the construction of constitutional provisions.

5. SR Bommai vs Union of India (1994)

- Court used the Preamble to declare Secularism as a basic feature of the Constitution. - Governments can’t promote any particular religion in India.

Conclusion

The Preamble is not just symbolic — it is an important tool for constitutional interpretation. It defines the spirit, values, and the guiding light of Indian democracy.
