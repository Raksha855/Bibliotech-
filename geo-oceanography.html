Oceanography

"Oceans are not just water — they are climate makers, heat movers, and biodiversity protectors." — Bibliotech

Introduction

Oceanography is the study of physical and biological features of oceans, including ocean currents, sea waves, tides, ocean floor topography, salinity, and marine life.

Ocean Floor Features

  • Continental Shelf: Shallow extension of continents
  • Continental Slope: Steep descent beyond the shelf
  • Abyssal Plains: Flat deep ocean floor
  • Mid-Ocean Ridges: Underwater mountain chains (e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge)
  • Ocean Trenches: Deepest parts (e.g., Mariana Trench)

Ocean Salinity

  • Average ocean salinity: 35‰ (parts per thousand)
  • Factors: Evaporation ↑ salinity, precipitation & river inflow ↓ salinity
  • High salinity: Subtropical belts (20–30° latitudes)
  • Low salinity: Equator and polar areas

Waves & Tides

  • Waves: Caused by wind action
  • Tides: Periodic rise/fall due to moon's gravity
  • Types of Tides: Spring Tide, Neap Tide
  • Daily Tides: 2 high + 2 low per day

Ocean Currents

Ocean currents are large-scale water movements caused by wind, Earth’s rotation, salinity and temperature differences.

Upwelling

  • Cold nutrient-rich water rises to surface → good for fisheries
  • Found in Peru, Canary, Benguela coasts

Coral Reefs

  • Formed by calcium carbonate skeletons of marine polyps
  • Types: Fringing, Barrier, Atoll
  • Threats: Coral bleaching, ocean acidification, pollution

Marine Resources

  • Biotic: Fish, crustaceans, algae
  • Abiotic: Salt, minerals, polymetallic nodules
  • Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) up to 200 nautical miles
