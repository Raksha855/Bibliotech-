Society & Economy in Medieval India

"Civilizations rise not just on conquests, but on the roots of their society and the rhythms of their economy." — Bibliotech

Social Structure During Medieval Period

1. Hierarchical Structure

  • Delhi Sultanate: Society divided among nobles, artisans, peasants, slaves.
  • Mughals: More stratified — nobles (mansabdars), zamindars, cultivators, artisans, labors.
  • Slavery: Common under Delhi Sultanate; abolished by Akbar (mostly symbolic).

2. Religious Composition

  • Hindus remained the majority; Muslims, Jains, Sikhs, Christians coexisted.
  • Syncretic culture: Bhakti and Sufi movements promoted religious tolerance.
  • Ulemas: Influential Islamic scholars shaping law and society.

3. Women in Society

  • Delhi Sultanate: Women largely confined; veil (purdah) system strengthened.
  • Mughals: Empresses like Nur Jahan held political power; education among elite women.
  • Practices like sati, child marriage, and dowry were prevalent among Hindus.

4. Urban Society

  • Flourishing towns: Delhi, Agra, Lahore, Hampi.
  • Specialized markets: Sarai (inns), bazaars, mandis.
  • Guild-based artisan communities (weavers, potters, blacksmiths).

Medieval Economy of India

1. Agriculture

  • Backbone of economy; land tax major revenue source.
  • Improved irrigation: wells, tanks, canals (especially under Vijayanagara).
  • Introduction of crops: sugarcane, rice varieties, tobacco (under Mughals).

2. Land Revenue System

  • Delhi Sultanate: Iqta system; officers (muqtis) given land to collect taxes.
  • Mughals: Zabt system (standardized assessment); Todar Mal's reforms.
  • Jagirdari & Zamindari systems coexisted — often led to peasant exploitation.

3. Trade & Commerce

  • Vibrant inland and maritime trade; cities like Surat, Cambay, Bengal ports thrived.
  • Trade with Arabs, Turks, Portuguese, and Central Asians.
  • Customs houses (Shahna): Regulated trade and collected duties.

4. Currency & Coinage

  • Delhi Sultanate: Silver tanka, copper jital — first regular coinage.
  • Mughals: Rupiya (silver) by Sher Shah; Akbar introduced uniform coinage.
  • Gold (mohur), silver (rupiya), copper (dam) coins widely circulated.

5. Industries & Crafts

  • Textiles, metalwork, pottery, perfumes, jewelry were main industries.
  • Silk weaving centers in Bengal; carpet weaving in Kashmir & Lahore.
  • State patronage to artisans; some crafts exported abroad.
