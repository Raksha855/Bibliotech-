Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

Article 32 provides the right to move the Supreme Court for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights. It makes the rights effective and not merely symbolic.

Why is it important?

  • Empowers citizens to directly approach the Supreme Court
  • Makes Fundamental Rights justiciable (can be enforced in court)
  • Also called the “Heart and Soul of the Constitution” – Dr. Ambedkar

Writ Jurisdiction of the Supreme Court

The Supreme Court (Article 32) and High Courts (Article 226) can issue writs for the enforcement of rights.

Types of Writs:

  • Habeas Corpus: “To have the body” – to release a person from illegal detention
  • Mandamus: “We command” – directs a public official/body to perform a duty
  • Prohibition: Issued to lower courts to stop them from exceeding their jurisdiction
  • Certiorari: Supreme Court transfers a case from lower court for review
  • Quo Warranto: “By what authority” – challenge someone’s right to hold a public office

Key Cases

  • Golak Nath vs State of Punjab (1967): Parliament cannot take away FRs
  • Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973): FRs are part of the Basic Structure
  • Minerva Mills Case (1980): Balance between Part III & IV is essential

Did You Know?

  • Article 32 is itself a Fundamental Right!
  • Supreme Court cannot refuse writ petition under Article 32
  • High Courts (Article 226) can issue writs even for legal rights (not just FRs)

Conclusion

Article 32 gives teeth to the Constitution and protects the soul of democracy by ensuring that Fundamental Rights are not violated.
